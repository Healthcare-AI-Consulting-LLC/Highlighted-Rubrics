Acute Lumbar Strain - SOAP Note

Patient: Marcus Johnson
Age/Sex: 38-year-old Male
Date: [Current Date]
Chief Complaint: "My lower back is killing me. I hurt it moving furniture yesterday."

SUBJECTIVE

History of Present Illness

38-year-old male warehouse supervisor presents with acute onset lower back pain that began yesterday afternoon while lifting a heavy dresser at work. He describes the pain as sharp and stabbing, localized to the lumbosacral region (slightly right-sided), currently rating it 5/10 at rest but increasing to 7-8/10 with movement. Pain has been constant since onset but significantly worsens with bending forward, twisting, lifting, or rising from a chair. He finds relief when lying supine with knees bent. Tried over-the-counter ibuprofen this morning with partial relief. Denies radiation of pain down the legs, numbness, tingling, or weakness in lower extremities. This is the first episode of this severity, though he reports minor back stiffness after long work days in the past that resolved with rest.

OLDCARTS

  • O - Onset: Started yesterday afternoon (acute onset ~24 hours ago); sudden onset while lifting heavy dresser at work
  • L - Location: Lumbosacral region (lower back around belt line), slightly right-sided; does not radiate beyond lower back
  • D - Duration: Constant since onset (~24 hours); does not come and go
  • C - Character: Sharp and stabbing with movement; occasional muscle spasm sensation when attempting to move
  • A - Aggravating/Alleviating: Aggravating: Bending forward, twisting, lifting, rising from chair. Alleviating: Lying supine with knees bent, ibuprofen 400mg (partial relief)
  • R - Radiation: None - pain stays localized to lower back; does NOT shoot down legs
  • T - Timing: Constant presence since yesterday; worse with any movement or activity
  • S - Severity: 7-8/10 with movement (bending, twisting); 5/10 at rest when seated
  • Pertinent Positives: Acute mechanical trigger (lifting), localized lumbosacral pain, muscle spasm, worse with activity, relieved by rest, occupational risk factor
  • Pertinent Negatives: No leg radiation, no numbness/tingling, no leg weakness, no bowel/bladder dysfunction, no saddle anesthesia, no fever, no weight loss

ASSESSMENT AND PLAN

Problem Statement

38-year-old male with no significant past medical history presenting with acute lumbar strain (mechanical low back pain) following occupational lifting injury, characterized by localized lumbosacral pain with muscle spasm, no radiculopathy, and no red flag symptoms. Examination reveals significant paraspinal muscle spasm and tenderness at L4-S1 with limited range of motion due to pain, but notably normal neurological examination (5/5 strength all groups, intact sensation, 2+ reflexes symmetric) and negative straight leg raise bilaterally.

Assessment Reasoning

The diagnosis of acute lumbar strain is strongly supported by: (1) Acute mechanical trigger - clear history of heavy lifting with sudden onset pain; (2) Pain pattern - localized lumbosacral pain that worsens with activity and improves with rest (classic mechanical pain); (3) Physical exam findings - paraspinal muscle tenderness and spasm (L4-S1), limited ROM due to pain; (4) Negative radiculopathy screening - no leg radiation, negative SLR, normal neurological exam rules out nerve root compression; (5) Negative red flags - no bowel/bladder dysfunction, no saddle anesthesia, no leg weakness, no fever, no weight loss, age <50; (6) Occupational risk factor - warehouse work is well-established risk for mechanical low back pain.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Most Likely: Acute Lumbar Strain (Mechanical Low Back Pain) - Supported by acute mechanical trigger, localized lumbosacral pain without radiation, paraspinal muscle spasm on exam, mechanical pain pattern, negative SLR, normal neuro exam, no red flags
  • Alternative: Lumbar Radiculopathy (Sciatica) - Less likely; ruled out by absence of leg radiation below knee, negative straight leg raise test, completely normal neurological examination
  • Unlikely: Lumbar Spinal Stenosis - Very unlikely given acute onset (stenosis typically chronic/progressive), age <50, no neurogenic claudication pattern
  • Must Rule Out: Cauda Equina Syndrome - Ruled out by negative red flag screen: no bowel/bladder dysfunction, no saddle anesthesia, no bilateral leg weakness, normal neurological exam
  • Must Rule Out: Spinal Epidural Abscess - Ruled out by absence of fever, no IV drug use, no systemic symptoms
  • Must Rule Out: Vertebral Compression Fracture - Ruled out by age <50, mechanism not consistent with fracture, no midline bony tenderness

PLAN

Problem #1: Acute Lumbar Strain (Mechanical Low Back Pain)

  • M - Measurement: Pain severity: 7-8/10 with movement, 5/10 at rest; Paraspinal muscle spasm L4-S1; Lumbar flexion limited to 30°; Unable to perform normal work duties
  • E - Evaluation: Acute presentation (~24 hours); Normal neuro exam rules out radiculopathy; Negative red flags; Tried OTC ibuprofen with partial relief only
  • A - Assessment: Uncomplicated acute lumbar muscle strain requiring conservative management; Expected natural history: 90% resolve within 4-6 weeks; Low risk for chronicity given age <40, but has risk factors (previous episode, occupational exposure, BMI 28.4)
  • T - Treatment:
    • Pharmacologic Management:
      • Ibuprofen 600 mg PO TID-QID PRN with food (first-line per ACP 2017; max 2400 mg/24 hours; duration 7-14 days PRN)
      • Acetaminophen 650-1000 mg PO TID-QID PRN if NSAIDs contraindicated (max 3000 mg/day)
      • AVOID opioids - NOT indicated per CDC 2022 guideline
    • Non-Pharmacologic Management:
      • STAY ACTIVE - AVOID BED REST (bed rest >2 days is HARMFUL and delays recovery; encourage normal activities as tolerated)
      • Work restrictions: Light duty for 2 weeks - no lifting >20 lbs, avoid bending/twisting
      • Ice/Heat: Ice first 48 hours, heat after 48 hours
      • Physical Therapy: Refer if no improvement at 4-6 weeks
    • Proper Body Mechanics:
      • Bend at knees, not waist when lifting
      • Keep objects close to body
      • Avoid twisting while lifting
      • Use mechanical aids when available

Diagnostics Ordered

None - Clinical diagnosis of acute lumbar strain

Imaging NOT indicated: Per ACP 2017 guidelines, routine imaging (X-ray or MRI) is NOT recommended for acute low back pain without red flags. Imaging does NOT improve outcomes and 90% of cases resolve within 6 weeks. Will consider imaging if symptoms persist >6 weeks or red flags develop.

Patient Education

Explained acute lumbar strain pathophysiology: Microscopic tears in paraspinal muscles from lifting injury causing inflammation and muscle spasm. Reassured this is common, self-limited condition - NOT a serious spinal injury.

  • Natural history: Most acute low back pain improves within 4-6 weeks; 90% resolve with conservative care
  • Why no imaging: X-rays and MRI do not improve outcomes; imaging only needed if red flags develop or no improvement after 6 weeks
  • CRITICAL: Staying active speeds recovery - bed rest is HARMFUL
  • Timeline: Expect 50% improvement by 2 weeks; full recovery by 4-6 weeks

Return Precautions

Return to clinic or call immediately if:

  • Loss of bowel or bladder control → GO TO ED IMMEDIATELY (cauda equina emergency)
  • Numbness in groin/buttocks area → GO TO ED IMMEDIATELY
  • New or worsening leg weakness → CALL CLINIC IMMEDIATELY
  • Pain radiating down leg below knee → CALL CLINIC
  • Fever, chills → CALL CLINIC
  • Severe worsening pain → CALL CLINIC
  • No improvement after 2 weeks → CALL CLINIC

Follow-Up

Return in 2-4 weeks or sooner if not improving or symptoms worsen

Monitoring Plan

At follow-up visit (2-4 weeks):

  • Reassess pain level and functional status
  • Monitor ROM and muscle spasm
  • Evaluate response to treatment
  • If improving: Continue current management, progress activity
  • If not improving: Consider PT referral, re-evaluate for red flags, consider imaging if >6 weeks, consider spine specialist referral

Return-to-work plan: Week 1-2: Light duty (no lifting >20 lbs); Week 3-4: Gradual increase; Week 4-6: Full duty clearance if pain resolved
