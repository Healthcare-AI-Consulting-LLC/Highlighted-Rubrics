Sample SOAP Note

Patient: Daniel Ramirez
Age/Sex: 16-year-old Male
Date: 2025-12-31
Chief Complaint: "I've had these pimples on my face for a few months now and they won't go away. It's really embarrassing."

SUBJECTIVE

History of Present Illness

16-year-old healthy male presents with a 6-month history of progressive facial acne. Breakouts began around age 15, initially mild but have gradually worsened despite OTC treatment with benzoyl peroxide wash and salicylic acid pads. He describes pimples with some having white tops, some red bumps, and occasional larger painful bumps under the skin. Acne is primarily on his face (forehead, cheeks, chin) with some spots on upper back. He is increasingly distressed about his appearance, rating embarrassment as 7/10, and has declined social events including a school dance.

OPQRST

  • O - Onset: Started approximately 6 months ago, around age 15-16; gradual onset
  • P - Provocation/Palliation: Worse with stress (especially during exams) and sweating from basketball; tried OTC benzoyl peroxide wash 2x/day and salicylic acid pads with minimal improvement
  • Q - Quality: "Pimples" - some with white tops, some red bumps, occasional larger painful bumps under the skin
  • R - Region/Radiation: Face (forehead, cheeks, chin) and upper back; no spread to other areas
  • S - Severity: Embarrassment 7/10; significantly affecting social interactions and self-esteem
  • T - Timing: Gradually worsening over past few months; constant presence with intermittent flares during stress
  • Pertinent Positives: Worsening with stress and sweating, occasional picking at lesions, family history of paternal teenage acne, failed OTC therapy, psychosocial impact
  • Pertinent Negatives: No new medications, no supplement or steroid use, no signs of systemic illness, no depression or suicidal ideation

Review of Systems

  • Constitutional: Denies fever, weight changes, fatigue
  • Skin: Facial breakouts as described; no rashes elsewhere; no hair loss
  • Psychiatric: Reports embarrassment and self-consciousness; denies depression, anxiety, or suicidal ideation
  • Endocrine: No excessive sweating, no heat/cold intolerance

SAMPLES

  • S - Surgical History: None
  • A - Allergies: NKDA
  • M - Medications: None; no supplements or steroids
  • P - Past Medical History: None; no history of eczema or atopic conditions
  • L - Lifestyle/Last Oral Intake: Active, plays basketball; washes face 2x/day; admits to occasionally picking at lesions
  • E - Events Leading to Presentation: Worsening acne despite OTC treatments; upcoming school dance causing increased concern about appearance
  • S - Social History:
    • Tobacco: Denies
    • Alcohol: Denies
    • Drugs: Denies
    • Sexual History: Not obtained (not clinically relevant)
    • Social Determinants: Lives with both parents; 10th grade student; good academic performance but stressed about upcoming tests; significant psychosocial impact - avoids eye contact, declined school dance invitation, spending more time alone

Family History: Father had moderate acne as teenager that improved in his 20s; no family history of severe/scarring acne or hormonal disorders

Immunizations: Up to date per school requirements

Well-appearing 16-year-old male in no acute distress; appears self-conscious, avoids eye contact when discussing acne; Tanner stage 4, appropriate pubertal development

Targeted Physical Exam (Skin)

Inspection:

  • Forehead: Multiple open comedones (blackheads) and closed comedones (whiteheads), 5-6 small erythematous papules
  • Cheeks (bilateral): Inflammatory papules and pustules, ~15-20 lesions per side with erythematous base
  • Nasal bridge: Scattered open comedones
  • Chin: Cluster of papules and pustules, 2 small nodules (3-4mm)
  • Perioral area: Spared
  • Upper back: Scattered comedones and papules, fewer than face

Pertinent Negatives: No cystic lesions; no purulence, warmth, or spreading erythema suggesting secondary infection; no truncal nodules or cysts; perioral sparing

Scarring Assessment: Mild post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation at sites of resolved lesions; no atrophic scars (ice pick, boxcar, rolling); no hypertrophic or keloid scarring

Acne Severity Classification: MODERATE - mixed comedonal and inflammatory lesions, >20 inflammatory lesions on face, 2 small nodules, truncal involvement

Screening Physical Exam

  • HEENT: Normocephalic, atraumatic; pupils equal and reactive; oropharynx clear
  • Cardiac: Regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs
  • Pulmonary: Clear to auscultation bilaterally, no respiratory distress

Labs: None obtained (not indicated for uncomplicated moderate acne vulgaris)

ASSESSMENT

Problem Statement

16-year-old male with no significant past medical history presenting with progressive moderate acne vulgaris with inflammatory component (papules, pustules, 2 nodules), unresponsive to OTC therapy, with associated psychosocial distress affecting quality of life. Examination reveals moderate acne with >20 inflammatory lesions per cheek, comedones on forehead/nose, and mild post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation.

Assessment Reasoning

The diagnosis of moderate acne vulgaris is supported by the age-appropriate presentation (adolescent male during puberty), classic lesion morphology (comedones, inflammatory papules/pustules, early nodules), distribution pattern typical of acne (face and upper back - sebaceous gland-rich areas), gradual onset and chronic course, and family history (genetic predisposition). The presence of >20 inflammatory lesions, 2 nodules, and truncal involvement classifies this as moderate severity. Failure of OTC treatment over 6 months indicates need for prescription combination therapy. The absence of comedones would suggest rosacea or folliculitis; their presence confirms acne vulgaris.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Most Likely: Acne Vulgaris (Moderate) - supported by age, classic morphology (comedones, papules, pustules, nodules), typical distribution, family history
  • Alternative: Folliculitis - less likely given presence of comedones, classic acne distribution, not purely follicular-based pattern
  • Unlikely: Rosacea - patient too young (typically >30), comedones present (absent in rosacea), no flushing or telangiectasias
  • Must Rule Out: Steroid-induced acne - ruled out by no steroid or supplement use confirmed, gradual onset, comedones present

PLAN

Problem #1: Acne Vulgaris (Moderate)

  • M - Measurement: >20 inflammatory lesions per cheek; 2 nodules (3-4mm) on chin; comedones on forehead/nose; scattered lesions on upper back; mild PIH; severity: MODERATE
  • E - Evaluation: Failed OTC benzoyl peroxide and salicylic acid x 6 months with minimal improvement; no prior prescription treatment
  • A - Assessment: Moderate inflammatory acne requiring prescription combination therapy; at risk for scarring given nodular component and picking behavior; significant psychosocial impact requiring attention
  • T - Treatment:
    • Topical Combination Therapy:
      • Adapalene 0.1% gel - apply thin layer to ENTIRE face at NIGHT (start every other night x 2 weeks, then nightly as tolerated; expect initial dryness/peeling)
      • Benzoyl peroxide 5% gel - apply in MORNING to affected areas (Caution: Can bleach fabrics)
      • Clindamycin 1% solution - apply to inflammatory lesions BID (ALWAYS use with benzoyl peroxide to prevent antibiotic resistance)
    • Oral Antibiotic: Doxycycline 100mg PO daily x 3 months (take with food and full glass of water; avoid lying down 30 minutes after taking; sun protection essential)
    • Skincare Recommendations:
      • Gentle non-comedogenic cleanser (Cetaphil, CeraVe) - avoid harsh scrubbing
      • Non-comedogenic moisturizer for treatment-related dryness
      • Daily sunscreen SPF 30+ (especially with retinoid and doxycycline)
      • Discontinue current OTC products to avoid over-drying
      • STOP picking at lesions - increases scarring and infection risk

Problem #2: Psychosocial Impact of Acne

  • M - Measurement: Embarrassment rated 7/10; avoiding social events; declined school dance; spending more time alone
  • E - Evaluation: No overt depression; no SI; appropriate for age but self-conscious
  • A - Assessment: Significant quality of life impact; warrants attention and monitoring
  • T - Treatment: Validation, reassurance, consider counseling referral if not improving with treatment

Diagnostics Ordered

None - clinical diagnosis

Consider hormonal workup only if treatment-resistant or signs of hyperandrogenism

Patient Education

Explained acne pathophysiology: Caused by hormones, clogged pores, and bacteria - NOT poor hygiene

  • Reassured that acne is very common in teenagers - not his fault
  • Discussed medication side effects: Dryness, peeling, and sun sensitivity expected with adapalene
  • Explained initial "purging" (temporary worsening) may occur in first 2-4 weeks
  • Set realistic timeline: Takes 6-8 weeks to see improvement - patience and consistency are key
  • Advised not to pop or pick lesions - leads to scarring and infection
  • Reassured that acne is not contagious
  • Validated concerns about appearance - acne can significantly impact quality of life at this age
  • Encouraged continued social activities

Return Precautions

Return or seek care if:

  • Severe new nodules or painful cysts developing
  • Signs of skin infection (increasing redness, warmth, pus, fever)
  • Signs of depression or significant mood changes
  • Severe allergic reaction to medications (rash, swelling, difficulty breathing)
  • Severe GI symptoms from doxycycline
  • No improvement after 8-12 weeks of consistent treatment

Follow-Up

Return in 6-8 weeks to assess treatment response

Monitoring Plan

Assess lesion count and severity at follow-up

  • Monitor for medication side effects (dryness, irritation, GI upset)
  • Screen for mood changes/depression at each visit
  • If inadequate response at 8-12 weeks:
    • Consider stronger retinoid (tretinoin 0.025%)
    • Dermatology referral for isotretinoin evaluation if progresses to severe/nodulocystic or develops significant scarring
